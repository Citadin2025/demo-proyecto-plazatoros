History

On January 9, 1910, with 36°C heat, the Plaza de Toros (Bullring) of Real de San Carlos was inaugurated with Spanish bullfighters the Torres brothers, "Bombita Grande" and "Bombita Chico." Eight thousand people filled the Plaza, arriving on the paddle steamers of the Mihanovich company: "Tritón," "Rivadavia," and "Colonia," which took five to six hours from Buenos Aires to Real de San Carlos.

Although the Uruguayan public was not as fond of bullfights, many people traveled from San José, Montevideo and other cities in Colonia by train, carriage and automobile. Earlier bullfights had been held in Colonia since 1895, and in 1908 a new project driven by Nicolás Mihanovich Jr. proposed the construction of a larger bullring as part of a tourist complex.

Construction of the Bullring

In 1909 Nicolás Mihanovich Jr. obtained permits to begin construction of the complex. Bricks were supplied by a nearby factory and the iron structure was imported from Great Britain and assembled on site. The bullring had a diameter of 100 meters, an arena of 50 meters and two tiers of stands; the main portico reached 22 meters in height. The project was completed in eight months.

The Bullring hosted bullfights, boxing, musical performances, comedy and other entertainments. It also included a chapel, restaurant, theater, infirmary and other facilities beneath the stands.

The Bullring Without Bullfights

After the prohibition of bullfights the complex continued to operate centered around the hotel's casino. In the 1920s, economic and regulatory problems reduced tourism. In 1935 there was an attempt to revive bullfights, but the Spanish Civil War and other issues frustrated the plans.

From 1943 onward, ownership passed to the Municipal Intendancy of Colonia and the complex gradually fell into disrepair; material theft and lack of maintenance accelerated deterioration. From 1957 car races were held around the Plaza until 1985 when they ceased for safety reasons.

Reconstruction Projects

Several restoration proposals were studied over the decades. A 1958 study looked at techniques used for the Colosseum, and in 1977 the Bullring was declared a National Historical Monument. Later attempts to secure funding stalled until a major restoration project began in 2019 and the plaza reopened to the public in 2021 after extensive work.
